# Kosmos Commons Finance Paper — Kindact Relevance Analysis

Source reviewed: https://www.kosmosjournal.org/kj_article/position-paper-a-commons-based-architecture-for-climate-finance/

## 1) Paper Summary

The paper argues that current climate-finance systems (carbon markets, green finance structures, blended finance) remain structurally extractive because they monetize ecosystems through ownership/speculation logics rather than stewardship logics.

It proposes a **commons trust architecture** with the following core mechanisms:

1. **Trusteeship over ownership**: key ecological assets are held in trust for present/future generations.
2. **Use-rights instead of commodified rights**: permits are time-bound, revocable, and non-transferable.
3. **Stewardship contributions (fees) instead of pure market pricing**.
4. **Fiduciary legal duties**: misuse/falsification is treated as breach of trust, enforceable through legal challenge.
5. **Open MRV + transparent digital ledgers** to track permits, restoration, and fund flows.
6. **Nested governance** from local to bioregional levels.
7. **CRC (Commons Reserve Currency) concept**: money issuance tied to measured ecological regeneration capacity.
8. **Dual-currency transition**: commons currency coexists with fiat for a long period during institutional transition.

## Appendix: Detailed Summary of the Kosmos Paper

**Title**: "Position Paper | A Commons-Based Architecture for Climate Finance: Closing the Economy–Ecology–Spirituality Gap"
**Author**: Fan Yang
**Published in**: Kosmos Journal, Volume 25 Issue 6
**Intellectual lineage**: Builds extensively on James Bernard Quilligan's foundational work on commons trusts and the Commons Reserve Currency (CRC).

### Part I — Why Current Climate Finance Fails and the Commons Alternative

**The core diagnosis** is that three decades of climate negotiations have failed to reduce emissions because the policy toolkit remains trapped inside the same intellectual architecture that caused the crisis. Carbon taxes, cap-and-trade, green bonds, and blended finance all operate within a debt-based, fossil-anchored monetary regime that structurally requires perpetual GDP growth. Even the petrodollar linkage is called out: because oil and energy commodities are traded and settled in dollars, the world's reserve currency is functionally tied to fossil energy, making the monetary system itself a barrier to decarbonization.

**Why markets dominate** — not because they are the best tool, but because they fit three systemic blind spots:

1. **Markets depoliticize complexity** — collapsing ecological and moral questions into a single metric (price), avoiding hard negotiations about responsibility.
2. **Markets externalize accountability** — offset mechanisms let actors purchase "permission to emit" and spread responsibility across intermediaries so no one is ultimately answerable.
3. **Markets appear borderless** — they move faster than treaties, but this speed often deepens vulnerability as corporations gravitate toward weaker regulation.

**The missing third pillar — the Commons.** Markets privatize, states centralize, neither commonizes. The paper frames the commons not as a shared resource but as the web of relationships, agreements, norms, and care practices through which people steward resources together. It argues all three domains — state, market, commons — must operate in dynamic balance. The state ensures equity and long-term vision; the market enables exchange and innovation; the commons grounds both in relationship, reciprocity, and ecological regeneration.

**The Commons Trust architecture** rests on seven interlocking pillars:

1. **The Commons as Shared Inheritance** — Planetary resources (atmosphere, oceans, forests, biodiversity) are held in trust, not owned. Beneficiaries include future generations and more-than-human communities.
2. **Trusteeship Instead of Ownership** — Trustees are local institutions (indigenous nations, communities, cooperatives) embedded in the ecosystems they steward. Usage rights are time-bound, non-transferable, and revocable — not tradable commodities.
3. **Commons Stewardship Fees, Not Speculative Markets** — Access to shared resources is governed by a regenerative contribution reflecting true ecological cost. Subsistence use is protected; commercial use pays fees. A portion of value generated is distributed as a social dividend (ecologically grounded UBI). Corporations receive only temporary, non-transferable use-rights conditioned on FPIC, ecological thresholds, and performance.
4. **Fiduciary Law and Common Rights** — Fiduciary law replaces contracts of sale with binding duties of care. Misuse of funds or falsification is a breach of trust, enforceable in court. Community proxies can challenge trustees legally.
5. **Polycentric and Nested Governance** — Multiple centers of decision-making, each with fiduciary duties. Every commons has a Social/Commons Charter (a localized eco-social contract). Local and regional trusts federate upward. At the global level, the UN Trusteeship Council could be revived for planetary commons.
6. **Dynamic Equilibrium Through Funding Mechanisms** — Commons budgets maintain shared principal; resource monetization is minimal and bounded; revenues circulate to communities, not to capital accumulation; extraction stays within ecological caps.
7. **Digital Coordination for Collective Stewardship** — Open, verifiable registries (blockchain or public digital ledgers) track use permits, restoration actions, and fund flows. DAOs, community tokens, and open-source protocols support transparency.

**Application — Amazon Bioregional Trust**: The paper works through a detailed scenario of how an Amazon Commons Trust would operate at basin, national, territorial, and community levels. Key mechanics include a Basin-Wide Amazon Commons Charter, a Basin Council integrating ecological science with indigenous knowledge to set bioregional thresholds, territorial trusts issuing time-bound non-transferable use permits, and commons contributions funding restoration, monitoring, guardian stipends, bioeconomy ventures, and cultural/spiritual site protection — all recorded on open digital ledgers. The architecture is designed to disrupt environmental crime by making it harder to launder illegal resources and by reducing economic dependence on illicit economies through stable, dignified livelihoods.

### Part II — The Commons Reserve Currency (CRC)

**The monetary diagnosis**: Climate finance cannot evolve while embedded in a monetary system detached from biophysical reality. Money is created through debt expansion and expectations of fossil-fueled growth. As fossil EROEI declines and ecosystems lose regenerative capacity, the money supply continues to expand, amplifying instability and ecological overshoot.

**CRC's core proposition**: Money should be created from ecological regeneration, not from interest-bearing debt. Value is anchored in the health, renewal, and carrying capacity of Earth's shared commons rather than in fossil energy, geopolitical power, or state creditworthiness.

**How CRC works**:

- A composite "planetary regeneration rate" replaces the interest rate as the universal reserve base. When ecosystems heal, CRC supply expands; when they degrade, issuance contracts.
- Issuance happens at the **bioregion** level. Each bioregion has an Ecological Commons Trust (ECT) that calculates annual CRC issuance capacity through a composite ecological regeneration index (forest regrowth, watershed recovery, soil carbon, biodiversity, atmospheric restoration, pollution reduction).
- The ECT allocates CRC downward to sectoral ecological sub-trusts (forest, river, soil, biodiversity), territorial indigenous/community trusts, and non-ecological commons trusts (health, digital, knowledge, culture).
- All trusts "rent" their issuance capacity from the biosphere's allowable surplus, ensuring every activity remains grounded in Earth's carrying capacity.
- Market-sector actors (manufacturing, banking, logistics) pay commons stewardship fees in CRC to the relevant Commons Trust for their ecological impact.

**Non-ecological commons** (health, digital, cultural) receive allocated shares of CRC based on how their work supports long-term stewardship, cultural vitality, and relational integrity — but only ecological regeneration determines total issuance capacity.

**Macroeconomic implications**: CRC dissolves the hierarchy of sovereign currencies that forces Global South nations to earn or borrow foreign currency. Trade deficits and balance-of-payments crises disappear because all participating nations draw value from the same regenerative base. Trade shifts from extractive comparative advantage to bioregional complementarity. Global value chains reconfigure into "global regeneration networks" where degradation anywhere reduces issuance capacity for all linked trusts.

**Transition**: The paper envisions a dual-currency era where CRC coexists with fiat currencies for several decades, beginning with carefully designed bioregional pilots. Over time, as institutions learn and indicators mature, the center of gravity shifts from debt-based to regeneration-based architecture.

### Cultural and Relational Prerequisites

The paper acknowledges that the biggest barrier is not technical but cultural. Commons governance requires "relational literacy" — the ability to perceive interdependence, negotiate conflict, uphold accountability, and act from care. This capacity is alive in indigenous traditions but has not been practiced widely enough for planetary governance. The paper argues commons trust itself helps cultivate the capacities it requires: when communities receive dividends from regeneration and participate in decision-making, a different civic culture forms. It also notes that commons governance elevates fields marginalized by market logic — ecology, anthropology, cultural studies, peacebuilding, liberal arts.
