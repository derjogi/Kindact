## Will Ruddick — "World Game at the Edge" & Implications for Kindact

### Who Is Will Ruddick?

Will Ruddick is the founder of **Grassroots Economics Foundation** (est. 2010, Kenya-based), one of the most experienced practitioners of community currencies in the world. His core thesis: marginalized communities already have abundant resources — labor, skills, goods, care — but lack the *medium of exchange* to coordinate them. National currency is scarce in these communities, so economic activity stalls despite real capacity. His solution is **Community Asset Vouchers** — locally issued tokens backed by real commitments to provide goods and services — which allow communities to trade among themselves without depending on external cash.

Over 15 years, Ruddick's team has iterated from paper-based community currencies (Bangla-Pesa in Mombasa, 2013) through mobile-money-based systems to the current **Sarafu Network**, a live decentralized application with 26,600+ users, 290+ pools/communities, and 2,140+ vouchers operating primarily in Kenya. The intellectual framework underpinning all of this is **Commitment Pooling** — a protocol with four core functions: *curation* (which commitments are trusted), *valuation* (how they relate in value), *limitation* (how much can move), and *exchange* (settling actual swaps). More recently, Ruddick has been developing the **Cosmo-Local Credit (CLC) DAO** — a governance and routing layer that connects sovereign local pools globally through shared standards, insurance, and liquidity routing, without collapsing them into a single central system.

Ruddick's position, in essence: **build from the edges, not from the center.** Real economic data, real trust, and real coordination happen where people live and work. Global systems should emerge as federations of local ones, not as top-down platforms that local communities must conform to.

### Summary of "World Game at the Edge" (March 2026)

The article bridges Buckminster Fuller's **World Game** concept (a planetary coordination system for allocating resources to benefit all humanity) with Ruddick's own practical infrastructure. Key moves:

**1. Fuller's World Game, updated.** Ruddick draws on Josh Pang's recent thesis, which argues that Fuller's World Game can now be understood as four technical layers: a **database** (world resource inventory), a **map** (geospatial visualization), a **simulation** layer (modeling scenarios before committing resources), and a **game** layer (participatory, rule-based coordination with visible consequences). Pang's claim is that the technological conditions for building this now exist; the challenge is organizational, not technical.

**2. The socialist calculation debate, reframed.** Rather than reopening the old "central planning vs. free markets" binary, Ruddick positions the World Game as asking a deeper question: *how can complex societies coordinate truthfully, visibly, and with care across many scales at once?* He invokes Chile's **Cybersyn** project (1971–73) — a distributed economic decision-support system — as a historical precedent showing this desire is real, not utopian, but also that coordination infrastructure is always political infrastructure.

**3. The World Game must be built at the edge.** This is the article's central argument. In Fuller's era, a world inventory implied centralized institutions and mainframe computers. Today, the relevant data is generated, verified, and acted upon *where people actually live, farm, build, repair, care, and trade.* Edge computing means data is produced and processed close to reality. A top-down global database of official statistics is insufficient; the inventory must be **fed from below** — community reports, local commitments, fulfillment histories, ecological observations, inventories. This doesn't replace official data; it *thickens* it.

**4. Commitment Pooling as the World Game's cellular architecture.** Each commitment pool becomes a "cell" — a bounded, semi-permeable unit maintaining its own integrity while exchanging with others. Ruddick maps the commitment pooling protocol onto Pang's four layers:
- **Database** → registry of commitments, fulfillments, attestations, swap histories
- **Map** → GPS-linked and network-linked visualization of where commitments are made and fulfilled
- **Simulation** → price vectors, inventory levels, swap limits, routing models — what happens if liquidity is added here, or a bottleneck forms there?
- **Game** → public coordination where communities, stewards, guarantors, and users co-design the system through clear rules and visible consequences

**5. Curation markets for trust, not speculation.** Ruddick redefines "curation market" as a *structured social process* through which communities decide which commitments are reliable enough to enter shared coordination systems — not a speculative attention-trading mechanism. This is where human judgment is formalized: what should count, what should be admitted, under what conditions is it trusted.

**6. Obligation clearing and the Slovenia lesson.** The article highlights Slovenia's multilateral set-off system, where visible obligation networks allowed companies to clear debts against each other, saving up to 7.58% of GDP in liquidity. The principle: when obligations are visible as a graph, you can reduce liquidity needs through clearing rather than requiring fresh cash. A world inventory that can't see reciprocal obligations is incomplete.

**7. Sarafu Network as proof of concept.** Not a finished planetary system, but already a "partial world game at community scale" — with physical/social mapping, network visualizations, vouchers, pool data, published impact reports, and the four functions of curation, valuation, limitation, and exchange operating in practice.

**8. Cosmo-Local Credit as the investment/insurance layer.** CLC routes liquidity across pools, funds insurance, coordinates governance, and preserves pool sovereignty. The key design principle: *standards are shared globally; redemption, accountability, and stewardship remain local.* Actuarial judgment can be built from fulfillment histories, pool behavior, location data, demand pressure, and network topology — making capital and insurance allocation more accountable.

**9. Risks acknowledged.** The article doesn't shy from problems: steward capture, data falsification, exclusion of less powerful communities from curation, privacy concerns from GPS-linked reporting, insurance mechanisms reproducing inequality. Ruddick explicitly calls for recourse procedures, transparent audit trails, appeal pathways, and clear limits on who can see, price, and route what.

### Deep Dive: How Curation and Valuation Work in Practice

The four functions of commitment pooling (curation, valuation, limitation, exchange) are described abstractly above. Here's how **curation** and **valuation** actually work on the ground in Ruddick's system.

#### Curation: Deciding Whose Commitments to Trust

Curation is the **gatekeeping function** — the process of selecting which service providers' vouchers are allowed into a given pool. It is performed by **pool stewards**: named, accountable individuals or groups who manage a specific pool.

**What it looks like in practice:**

1. **Community formation first.** Before any digital system is involved, Grassroots Economics runs in-person workshops with local stakeholders — businesses, farmers, teachers, tradespeople. They explain commitment pooling, run physical simulation games (the "bean game," the "thread game"), and map the community's existing resources and needs. This social process is where trust is initially assessed: *who in this community actually provides reliable goods and services?*

2. **Voucher creation.** Each service provider creates their own voucher (Community Asset Voucher / CAV) on Sarafu Network. A voucher is essentially a formalized commitment — a digital "gift card" that says "I will provide X amount of goods/services to whoever holds this." A hairdresser might create vouchers worth the equivalent of $400 in haircuts; a bakery might create $400 in bread vouchers. The voucher declaration includes the type of promise, quantity, and redemption terms.

3. **Steward curation decision.** The pool steward then decides *which* of these vouchers to grant "drawing rights" into the pool. This is not automatic — it's a deliberate judgment call. The steward is asking: *Is this person's commitment credible? Can they actually deliver what they promise? Are their goods/services something the pool's community needs?* A steward might accept the bakery and the hairdresser but reject a service provider whose capacity is questionable or whose offering doesn't complement the existing pool.

4. **Ongoing auditing and dispute resolution.** Curation doesn't stop at the gate. Internal and external audits check whether commitments can reasonably be fulfilled. If a service provider fails to redeem their vouchers (i.e., someone shows up with bakery vouchers and gets turned away), mediators intervene. Stewards can withdraw a provider's access to the pool. The steward's own reputation is on the line — if they curate poorly, the pool loses trust and participants leave.

5. **Signed agreements.** The process is formalized through three agreements: an **Economic Commons Agreement** (terms of ecosystem participation), a **Voucher Declaration / Membership Agreement** (unit of account, redemption commitments, pool limits, expiration, mediation rules), and a **Service Agreement** with a core service provider like Grassroots Economics for platform access and arbitration.

**The key insight for Kindact:** Curation is fundamentally a **social trust assessment performed by named, accountable stewards**, not an algorithmic or crowd-voted process. It's closer to a credit committee than a popularity vote. The steward's judgment about *who is trustworthy* is the foundation everything else rests on. If curation fails — if unreliable commitments enter the pool — the entire system degrades.

#### Valuation: Setting Relative Worth Between Commitments

Once vouchers are curated into a pool, valuation determines **how they relate to each other in exchange value** — essentially, the price list.

**How it works:**

1. **Steward-set price index.** The pool steward establishes the relative value of each curated voucher. In Ruddick's book, this is illustrated simply: if a pool contains Apples, Bananas, and Carrots, the stewards might declare that 1 Apple = 1 Banana = 2 Carrots. This "price list" is what makes exchange possible — without it, there's no way to know how much of Voucher A you need to put in to take out Voucher B.

2. **Reference anchors.** In many practical cases, vouchers are denominated in the national currency (e.g., Kenyan Shillings). If the bakery's vouchers are worth KES 100 each and the hairdresser's are worth KES 200 each, the exchange rate follows directly: 2 bakery vouchers = 1 hairdresser voucher. When vouchers share a common denomination, valuation is straightforward — they can all be treated as "equal per unit of national currency."

3. **External data sources.** On the digital platform, external data sources can provide real-time reference prices for certain commitments (like stablecoins pegged to national currencies). Smart contracts can also be designed to adjust relative values based on parameters like seasonality, supply/demand within the pool, or other community-agreed factors.

4. **Steward adjustment over time.** Valuation is not fixed. Stewards can change relative prices as conditions shift — a drought might make food vouchers more valuable relative to service vouchers; seasonal gluts might reduce certain prices. The Ruddick book explicitly notes: *"This may be changed as needed by the pool stewards, taking into account factors such as seasonality."*

5. **Valuation determines the "unit of account."** The set of relative values within a pool implicitly creates a shared unit of account for that community. You don't need an external currency to have prices — the relative relationships *are* the prices. This is how ancient rotational labor systems worked: when one neighbor brings water and another works all day, the community needs some shared sense of relative worth to maintain fairness over time.

**What valuation is NOT:** It is not market-driven price discovery in the conventional sense. There's no order book, no bidding. It's a **steward-administered price**, closer to a managed exchange rate than a free market. The steward is making a judgment about fairness and balance, not maximizing efficiency. This is a deliberate design choice — Ruddick's system prioritizes **community-calibrated fairness** over the "efficiency" of spot pricing, because in small communities with limited liquidity, market pricing would be volatile and extractable.

**The tension:** Steward-administered pricing works well in small, high-trust pools where the steward knows local conditions intimately. It becomes harder as pools grow or connect across communities — which is exactly where Cosmo-Local Credit's routing and insurance layer comes in, and where algorithmic pricing assistance becomes necessary. Ruddick's book acknowledges this: *"For such assistance and optimizations to happen without coercion, and without extraction through centralization, the underlying AI or algorithms must be decentralized, adaptable, and capable of responding to the fluid nature of pool liquidity and valuation."*

#### How This Compares to Kindact's Approach

| Function | Ruddick / Sarafu | Kindact |
|----------|------------------|---------|
| **Who curates** | Named pool stewards with personal accountability | Community-at-large via voting + peer review |
| **What is curated** | Service providers' commitments (vouchers) entering a pool | Issues/proposals entering the decision pipeline; implementers' work being verified |
| **Curation criteria** | "Can this person actually deliver?" — trust & capacity | "Is this proposal net-positive?" — metrics & deliberation |
| **Who sets value** | Pool steward(s), manually | AI suggestion + community deliberation per issue |
| **Value anchoring** | National currency denomination or steward-set ratios | Global $CC (one token, one value everywhere) |
| **Value adjustment** | Steward discretion, seasonality, local conditions | Fluid voting can adjust rewards mid-implementation |
| **Accountability** | Steward reputation; they can be replaced if they curate poorly | Distributed — no single named person is accountable for verification quality |

**The gap Kindact might learn from:** Kindact's verification phase currently distributes accountability across peer review, third-party checks, and fraud controls — but nobody is *named and on the hook* the way a pool steward is. Ruddick's model suggests that having a designated, visible steward who curates quality (and whose reputation rises or falls with the pool's integrity) creates stronger accountability than purely distributed review. This doesn't mean Kindact needs to copy the steward model wholesale — but it's worth considering whether some form of **named verification stewardship** (perhaps per topic area or per community) would improve trust and reduce free-riding in the verification process.

### Implications for Kindact

Several of Ruddick's ideas resonate directly with Kindact's design, and some challenge it productively:

**1. "Edge-first" data vs. Kindact's top-down metrics.** Kindact's current design relies heavily on AI estimation, prediction markets, and expert input for its metrics/simulation layer. Ruddick's argument suggests Kindact should also think about how *implementers and communities themselves* feed structured data upward — not just as "implementation reports" for verification, but as living economic intelligence that other communities can learn from. Kindact already uses ValueFlows for implementation reports; this is a natural extension. **The learning: Kindact's metrics layer could be richer if it treated ground-level implementation data as a first-class input to simulation, not just as proof-of-work for reward minting.**

**2. Commitment Pooling's "four functions" as a lens for $CC design.** Ruddick's curation/valuation/limitation/exchange framework is a useful diagnostic for Kindact's token economy:
- **Curation**: Kindact has this (community deliberation decides what work is valuable). ✓
- **Valuation**: Kindact partially has this (AI suggestions + community deliberation set reward amounts), but lacks the relative-value-between-communities mechanism that Ruddick's pools handle through exchange rates.
- **Limitation**: Kindact has voter-scaled reward caps and demurrage, but doesn't have the concept of *pool-level limits* on how much can move — which is exactly what the local currencies analysis above wrestles with.
- **Exchange**: Kindact assumes a single global token, so exchange between communities is trivially 1:1 — which, per Ruddick, may be a feature (simplicity) but also a blindness (it hides real economic differences between communities).

**The learning: if Kindact eventually moves to Option C (local tokens + $CC as settlement layer), Ruddick's four-function framework is a ready-made design language for each local pool's economic governance.**

**3. Obligation clearing — a missing concept in Kindact.** Kindact currently thinks in terms of *mint-and-spend*: work gets done → $CC minted → $CC circulates. Ruddick's article highlights that *obligation clearing* (matching reciprocal commitments directly without needing fresh tokens) is a powerful liquidity-saving mechanism. If Community A owes Community B 100 $CC for consulting, and Community B owes Community A 80 $CC for data services, only 20 $CC needs to actually move. Kindact's architecture doesn't currently contemplate this — but if it grows to support inter-community trade, obligation clearing could dramatically reduce the liquidity bootstrapping problem. **The learning: keep this in mind as a Phase 3+ mechanism. It's especially relevant if local sub-currencies emerge.**

**4. Curation as social process, not just voting.** Kindact's "decide" phase uses voting, delegation, and consensus iteration. Ruddick's "curation" is subtly different — it's not about deciding *what to do* but about deciding *whose commitments to trust*. In Kindact terms, this maps most closely to the verification/dispute resolution phase (who is a credible implementer? whose implementation reports should be accepted?). Currently Kindact handles this through peer confirmation, third-party verification, and fraud controls. Ruddick's model suggests a more structured "stewardship" role — named individuals or groups who are explicitly responsible for curating the quality of work in their domain, with visible accountability. **The learning: consider whether Kindact's verification phase needs named "stewards" with visible track records, rather than purely distributed peer review.**

**5. The cellular metaphor — sovereignty without isolation.** This is perhaps the deepest resonance. Kindact's entire "parallel layer" philosophy (voluntary, non-replacing, complementary to existing governance) mirrors Ruddick's "cells" — bounded units that maintain integrity while exchanging with others. Ruddick's framing of "local sovereignty without isolation, and global interoperability without forced homogenization" could almost be a tagline for Kindact. The difference is that Ruddick builds from *economic* cells (commitment pools) while Kindact builds from *governance* cells (issue-based deliberation communities). **The learning: these may be complementary rather than competing approaches. A Kindact community could *use* commitment pooling as its economic layer, while the deliberation/decision/implementation cycle provides the governance layer that Ruddick's pools currently lack.** This is a potential integration point worth exploring with Grassroots Economics.

**6. "Infrastructure for coordination is always also political infrastructure."** Ruddick's Cybersyn reference carries a warning: any system that makes economic activity visible, routes resources, and formalizes trust relationships *is* a governance system, whether it calls itself one or not. Kindact already embraces this (it explicitly positions itself as governance infrastructure). But the warning applies in reverse too: Kindact's governance mechanisms will inevitably shape economic outcomes, even if $CC starts as "just" a reward token. **The learning: Kindact should treat its economic design as governance design from day one, not as a secondary technical detail.**

**7. Practical partnership potential.** Ruddick's infrastructure (Sarafu Network, CLC DAO, the commitment pooling protocol) is live, open-source, and already listed in Kindact's ecosystem partners. If Kindact's Option C (local tokens + $CC as settlement) ever materializes, building on Sarafu/CLC rather than from scratch is the obvious path. More immediately, Kindact communities could experiment with commitment pooling *within* the Kindact framework — using Kindact for deliberation and decision-making, and Sarafu-style pools for local economic coordination of the approved work. **The learning: this is a concrete collaboration opportunity, not just a theoretical alignment.**
