## According to Design Principles for CC (Community Currencies)

### Context

- **Economic Environment:** The currency is designed to address a "misconception" that there is not enough money to solve global problems; rather, it targets **inefficient redistribution** and the lack of a powerful entity to enforce it. It responds to an environment where **money has an overwhelming influence** on issues that should be implemented for the collective good, often favouring the rich over the poor.
- **Institutional Environment:** The system operates as a **voluntary, extra-governmental** framework. It does not seek to force governments into adoption but creates a parallel institutional arrangement where participation is **encouraged** by incentives, *not* *enforced* by laws & the executive.

### Goals

- **Purpose:** The primary purpose is to **incentivise "good deeds"** for humankind, such as addressing climate change, poverty, and conflict. It aims for a **paradigm shift** where entities are rewarded for outcomes (e.g., "saved lives") rather than just outputs (e.g., "distributed pills").
- **Scale and Scope:** The global currency ($CC) is **unlimited in scale**, intended for use from local neighbourhoods to global initiatives. The currency can furthermore be broken down into **regional interchangeable tokens**, such as $London and $Auckland tokens that can be traded within a smaller ecosystem, and each with the possibility of having special rules applied to promote localism or other desirable incentives. Regional tokens are exchangeable with the global token, but conversion fees might apply.

### **Mechanisms**

- **Governance (Shared/Decentralised):**
    - Currency mechanisms can be **adjusted by voting on issues**, same as any other issue on the platform (whose main purpose is to facilitate good decision making on different levels)
    - **Liquid Conviction Voting:** Users can vote directly or delegate their power to trusted others, with the ability to revoke this power at any time. Vote weight increases the longer it is held on a proposal to prevent impulsive decisions. Decisions are *active* based on the *approval ratio* (not the ratio of voters to total users) and usually don’t have deadlines
- **Architecture (Digital/Blockchain):**
    - The system utilizes a **distributed blockchain** to ensure all transactions, reports, and votes are transparent and censorship-resistant. It will be chain-agnostic to enable use of the best/appropriate technology with minimal effort and avoid lock-in
    - **Privacy:** It employs technologies like **zero-knowledge proofs** to ensure that while transactions are visible, individual voting and some types of transactions can remain anonymous
- **Features:**
    - **Issuance:** $CC are **freshly minted** and disbursed exclusively after a community-approved action is verifiably implemented
    - **Valuation:**
        - **No Backing:** There is no extrinsic backing or guarantees foreseen, although
        - **Status value:** $CC is gained by performing desirable community-approved tasks and thus is a direct indication of how much a user has **contributed to the community**
        - **Intrinsic utility:**  Users are required to **pay platform access fees in $CC** to be able to participate actively in shaping issues.
        - **Added utility:** With increasing adoption, $CC could be used to access various community resources and exchanged for goods at participating ventures
    - **Supply Control:** Fees paid to the platform are destroyed (token burning); and the system may include **token decay** (e.g., 1% per year)

.

◦ **Privileged Transactions:** Users can receive specific rewards for eco-friendly behaviours, such as using public transport or recycling

.

**4. Defining Evaluation Criteria**

- **Sustainability Assessment:** Proposals require a projected **impact assessment** using metrics such as environmental sustainability, social equity, and community well-being

.

- **Capacity for Change:** The currency is designed for **transformative capacity**, aiming to "radically change the way governance is done" by shifting the focus from party-based voting to fine-grained, issue-based participation

.

- **Adoption Levels:** The sources outline a phased adoption strategy:

◦ **"Seeding" (0-10 users):** Where the currency's value is primarily based on **reputation**

.

◦ **"Sprouting" (10-100 users):** Where the token gains **marketplace utility** and is used for borrowing, lending, and paying for services

.

To understand this currency system, imagine a **reward card for the planet**: instead of earning points for spending money at a shop, you "earn" currency by proving you've done something the community values, and you then use those points to pay your "community rent" (access fees) or trade them for local goods.

## Examples

### Stage1: Seeding (0 - 10 users)

(No other Seeds are in circulation yet. This is likely the most difficult phase to get it bootstrapped)

**Local example: Bakery**

A bakery in a community offers unhealthy bread for lower price than healthy, the community wants to change that and make healthy wholemeal bread cheaper.

They create an issue on the platform, and decide to reward bakers with $CC for healthy bread, but not for unhealthy bread, to encourage them to shift their production & eventually hopefully make healthy bread cheaper. For each kg of wholemeal flour the baker uses in their bread, the baker would receive 1 $CC.

In the very beginning, the only ‘value’ associated with $CC is *reputation*: The more $CC the bakery has, the healthier their bread (averaged). 

This is of low effect, but the effort is also low to receive these $CC. Some bakeries start to submit their receipts for their wholemeal flour to the platform and immediately receive $CC, without changing anything else (i.e. the actual ratio of white vs wholemeal flour + the price of goods will still remain the same). They also start to display a counter of how many $CC they received. (And users can also see this information in their platform app).

The community, being aware of the significance of $CC in this case (many of them would have voted on the platform), will now start to frequent the bakery displaying the highest $CC just a little bit more, very gradually increasing those bakeries reputation & profits. More bakeries pick up on this, and start doing similar, creating a (slight) competitive drive to increase wholemeal use, and enabling them to slightly reduce their price for wholemeal products.

Scenario if the community is small and there is only one bakery:

In this case, there might not be much/any incentive for the bakery to adapt the $CC system, as they won’t have a ‘competitive advantage’ and can only reduce the price of their wholemeal bread if they can get the wholemeal flour cheaper, or if they get some other advantage.

The same community also has a small community garden, with a few seasonal fruits and vegetables every now and then. In the past, they gave access to this produce only to those that helped maintain the garden, but now they decide they want to give access to all those that contribute to the community in *some* way, not only through gardening. 
Because $CC is given to those that in some way do what the community decides is good, they decide to use $CC to give access to garden produce, along with some $CC rewards for those helping in the garden. They set a price on produce in $CC that seems appropriate - let’s say 5 $CC for each unit of produce. Or they might decide that any $CC holder will have ‘access’ to the garden. This effectively gives a value to $CC tokens, even though not very stable and with rather little ‘liquidity’.

The bakery might like to have access to the community garden themselves, or they anticipate someone else might want to have access, so they opt into the wholemeal issue and start to earn $CC, and start selling it to their customers that want to have access to the garden but can’t contribute in other ways or can’t (yet) earn $CC themselves.

### Stage2: Sprouting (10 - 100 users)

More and more issues are being created in a similar way to give $CC for different purposes: Planting Trees, mowing the lawn in that empty slot of land that’s an eyesore to everyone, offering healthier products. 

This of course causes price inflation, but at the same time things are getting done, people get more involved with the community (they have more and more insights in the wants and needs), and $CC gets at least more known. $CC might be perceived as something without value, but nevertheless something that’s good for the community.

More issues will be created on the platform, and $CC might start to have more utility. Some people might start offering things on a $CC ‘marketplace’: with their community they decide that it’s desirable to lend & borrow more things to each other. For each thing that you list on the platform you can get some initial $CC, and every time it gets borrowed you might require the borrower to pay or deposit some amount of $CC tokens. They might also decide to have a community toolshed and charge $CC or the local currency for access, the local $ can be used to replace tools or buy new ones.

At a certain point (usage based), the platform starts to charge access fees - in $CC. E.g. creating and participating in 3 issues per month is free for each user, but after that every user has to pay a monthly amount of $CC in order to participate more (they can still read, but not contribute without paying). Those $CC paid to the system will be ‘burnt’ (destroyed/deleted).  

By now $CC might be a bit more commonly available in the community, and some people might start to exchange it; possibly the bakery will see enough value to accept $CC to sell bread.

## Working Notes: Addressing Economist Concerns

### A. What is $CC economically? (Primary Identity)

**Hierarchy of roles:**
1. **Reward unit (primary):** $CC is fundamentally an incentive mechanism to motivate community-approved work — a "rival" to purely neocapitalist market incentives. Value flows to those who do good.
2. **Reputation signal (secondary):** Accumulated $CC signals past contribution. Unlike pure reputation systems, $CC is transferable — but this is a feature, not a bug: when you buy $CC from someone, you're directly supporting a contributor.
3. **Tradeable medium (tertiary):** Tradeability enables the reward and reputation functions to work. Contributors can convert effort into goods/services; supporters can fund contributors.
4. **Access right (quaternary):** Platform access fees create a utility sink, but this is infrastructure, not core identity.

**Addressing the "buyable reputation" objection:**
Economists may argue that tradeable reputation undermines trust. Counter-argument: the system distinguishes between *earned $CC* (visible in transaction history as minted-from-work) and *purchased $CC*. The blockchain ledger makes provenance transparent — you can always see whether someone earned or bought their tokens.
But regardless of that, anyone who buys $CC is supporting the community, which could be argued is a reputable action in itself. If one billionaire funds the work of 1000 people but doesn't do any of the work themselves, the billionaire still deserves a good chunk of the reputation.

### B. Supply Discipline: Reactive Sinks over Hard Caps

**Core argument:** If every $CC minted represents verified real-world value creation (MMT framing), there's no inherent inflation problem. Hard caps could perversely discourage good work when limits are reached.

**Approach: Dynamic taxation (reactive sinks)**

Four mechanisms adjust algorithmically based on circulation metrics:

| Mechanism | Tax Analogy | Adjustment Trigger |
|-----------|-------------|-------------------|
| Demurrage rate | Wealth tax | High mint/burn ratio → faster decay |
| Transaction fees | VAT / sales tax | High velocity or manipulation signals |
| Access fees | Service/licensing fees | High platform activity |
| Verification threshold | Regulatory compliance | Large reward requests require more scrutiny |

**Why this works for economists:** It's transparent, automated taxation — rates respond to observable metrics rather than political negotiation. Central banks adjust interest rates; Kindact adjusts sink rates.

**Soft issuance guardrails (bookmarked for later detail):**
Reward amounts are specified during Deliberation. To unlock larger rewards, proposals need proportionally more voter support — e.g., each additional voter multiplies the available reward cap by ~1.5×. This creates natural scaling where small local issues need few supporters, while large global initiatives require broad consensus. Global scope issues have higher caps than local ones.

### C. Reward Pricing: Who Decides How Much?

**Initial proposal:** Issue creator *can* specify an amount, but AI also *suggests* a reward based on comparable tasks, scope, and impact estimates. The community adjusts/approves via normal deliberation.

**Competitive bidding (optional):**
- For mutually exclusive tasks (building a singular object), implementers could bid on price
- Not core to platform — negotiation often happens outside anyway
- Platform provides **"intention to implement"** indicator so participants see if there's competition

**Non-exclusive issues:**
- Multiple implementers can work in parallel (e.g., battery research by multiple teams)
- All get paid based on progress reports, regardless of who "wins"
- Encourages collaboration and parallel exploration

**Preventing self-dealing:**

| Mechanism | How it works |
|-----------|--------------|
| Voter-scaled caps | Lone actor can only award themselves minimal amounts; large rewards need many supporters |
| Asymmetric voting | Negative votes reduce the cap more than positive votes increase it (e.g., 1 objection = 2+ approvals) |
| Fluid voting | Rewards can be adjusted mid-implementation; community can catch abuse 1-2 months in and slash future payments |
| Retroactive penalties | Fraud detection → banned from proposing amounts, or full platform ban |

B. Put issuance on a budget (avoid “infinite mint if you can pass votes”)
“Mint only for verified work” does not by itself limit supply. If the community approves too many tasks or overestimates impact, you get runaway issuance.

Add an explicit epoch budget mechanism:

Time is divided into epochs (e.g., monthly).
The protocol sets a max issuance per epoch (a monetary policy parameter).
Each issue draws from that budget via an agreed reward schedule.
Minimal version (credible and simple):

Issuance cap: ( M_t \le \bar{M}_t ) each epoch.
Reward allocation: issues compete for a fixed pool; reward amounts are bounded.
This converts “proof of work” into “proof of work within a budget constraint,” which economists expect.

C. Specify how rewards are priced (a reward function, not just “community-approved”)
Economists will immediately ask: Who sets the reward size? How do you prevent vote-buying / self-dealing / optimism bias?

Bounty posting: proposals include a requested bounty; approval requires a vote and a “cost reasonableness” check, or competing bids from implementers.
Competitive bidding/auction: implementers bid for the smallest $CC reward they’ll accept for the milestone. Community selects bid + quality.
Impact-score schedule: reward = base units × verified impact score, with caps.
Even a short statement like “Rewards are determined via a capped bounty + competitive bids + moderator/expert review” materially increases credibility.

Proposer stake (returned if issue passes basic checks; partially slashed for spam/fraud).
Implementer stake (slashed for falsified reports).
Verifier stake (verifiers are randomly sampled; slashed if caught rubber-stamping).

A natural token sink (staking demand)
A deterrent against collusion

Milestones have objective acceptance criteria (ex ante).
Random audits with some probability (p), increasing with reward size/risk.
Challenge period: anyone can dispute within N days by posting a bond.
Arbitration: a designated jury/panel (could be elected, rotating, or specialist-certified).
Bond/stake slashing funds the arbitration cost.
This is standard for “pay for results” systems and directly addresses Goodhart’s Law/gaming.

Demurrage applies per verified identity, not per wallet address (prevents splitting funds across wallets to stay under the floor).

3) Rationale and trade-offs (what economists will care about)
Budget constraint + reward pricing turns this from an inspirational story into a system with macro discipline. Without it, “mint on verified work” is still politically endogenous money creation.
Staking/slashing + audits is the canonical solution to principal–agent problems in decentralized reward systems.
Treasury funding is necessary for credible enforcement and continuous platform maintenance. “Burn everything” reads ideologically pure but operationally fragile.
Separating reputation from money prevents a classic failure mode: markets buying legitimacy.
Alternative (materially different): make $CC explicitly a voucher/redeemable credit for concrete services (API credits, audit credits, event access). That provides a harder value anchor but is more centralized/operationally heavy. For now, the simple path above is enough.

Excluding low-liquidity contributors: “pay only after completion” can exclude those without upfront capital → mitigate via milestone payments, escrow, community advance pools, or external sponsors.
Demurrage avoidance: wallet splitting / custodianship games → mitigate by applying demurrage at the identity level, and limiting custodial aggregation.
