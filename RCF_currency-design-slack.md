Hi all! Appreciating this conversation (even though I only intermittently am getting to catch up here). And thank you, @Basma Symington & @Leanne for inviting me to share bits of what I've got to offer!

I personally get really excited about any system that authentically treats the well-being of people and nature as central design priorities, and when it comes to currencies I feel excited about ones that are backed by value in the real economy and that self-fund universal basic services. 

So I like geeking out about currencies which:
Are generated into circulation to represent & simultaneously fund the value generation that comes from completing essential work and meeting basic needs (as defined by communities themselves - a key shared-governance opportunity as we discussed on last week's call)
Link units of measurement with person-centered metrics of need fulfillment (e.g. "Day", "Week", "Month", & "Year" units of measurement link to the amount of new currency generated to pay those who meet an average person's needs in the corresponding amount of time)

Scale in a person-centered modular economy. This covers the costs of scalably meeting the needs of any number of people with a stable rate of demurrage without any added cost for other members of the economy, limiting scale instead based on the acceptance of the currency for real exchanges
Balance currency strength/velocity by modulating spending and saving pressure through demurrage (spending pressure) and transaction fees (spending friction)

A compost-backed local token system


Consider a local token system backed by compost.

A central compost warehouse functions like a local central bank. It has a monopoly on the supply of certified CSA regenerative compost and guarantees that 1 token can always be redeemed for 1 bag of compost. This redemption promise gives the token its fundamental backing.

Separately, there is a community group issuing tokens freely, without regard to compost supply or redemption capacity. As more tokens circulate, the token becomes abundant relative to demand, and real  value in U.S. dollars begins to fall. E.g positive balances of the token build up and when I go to the store to spend they increasingly take a smaller and smaller percentage of them - LIke in the Berkshare case pre 2022, like in the  HVC case - until nobody in town wants them because they can't spend them. Their effective value in dollars is falling (even if the agreed reference price is fixed at 2:1 tokens:USD).

Meanwhile, a local greenery store sells the same compost for USD $0.50 per bag.

The exchange-rate target and the arbitrage mechanism

The compost bank wants to maintain a target exchange rate of:

1 token ≈ USD $0.50

Here’s how that target is defended automatically:

If the community token trades below USD $0.50 (for example, USD $0.40 per token),
arbitrage becomes profitable.

An arbitrager can:

Use 1 token to redeem 1 bag of compost from the compost bank
Sell that compost to the greenery store for USD $0.50
This yields a risk-free profit.
As arbitragers repeat this process:
Tokens are removed from circulation (they are returned to the compost bank)
Compost leaves the warehouse
The excess supply of tokens shrinks
The token’s market value is pushed back up toward USD $0.50 
What this achieves (and what it reveals)

This mechanism means that:
USD $0.50 becomes a price floor for the token
The token cannot sustainably fall below the compost redemption value
The backing asset (compost) and redemption promise discipline the token supply

However, this also reveals a key vulnerability:
If tokens are issued faster than compost can be supplied,
the compost bank will eventually run out of compost
At that point, redemption stops, arbitrage collapses, and
the “peg” to USD $0.50 fails

The deeper monetary lesson
This system works not because of agreements, belief or declarations, but because: The central warehouse stands ready to target and exchange tokens for a real good at a fixed rate. That is what makes the exchange rate defensible.

If token issuance is not constrained by roughly matching it to compost production or replenishment overtime, the system becomes unstable — not because arbitrage fails, but because the real backing asset is exhausted.

One-sentence summary
A compost-backed token can maintain a target USD exchange rate through arbitrage as long as redemption into compost is credible and supply-constrained; uncontrolled token issuance eventually breaks that credibility.
